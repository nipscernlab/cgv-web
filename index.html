About CGV Web

The Calorimeter Geometry Viewer is a browser-native 3D tool for exploring particle physics detector data from the ATLAS experiment at CERN's Large Hadron Collider.

It renders individual calorimeter cells — the ~190,000 readout units that measure energy deposited by particles emerging from proton–proton collisions. Each cell lights up in proportion to the energy it absorbed, letting physicists identify jets, electromagnetic showers, and hadronic activity at a glance.

The tool runs entirely in your browser: XML event data is parsed by a Rust/WebAssembly module and rendered via Three.js with custom GLSL shaders. Nothing is uploaded to any server.

NIPS Signal Processing Lab

The Nuclear Instrumentation and Signal Processing Laboratory (NIPS) is based at the Federal University of Juiz de Fora (UFJF), Minas Gerais, Brazil. Since 2008 the group has collaborated formally with the ATLAS detector operations team at CERN, focusing on calorimeter signal processing and energy reconstruction.

Core research areas include optimal filtering algorithms for pulse shaping in the TileCal readout, FPGA-based real-time trigger systems, and machine-learning methods for noise suppression in liquid-argon calorimeters.

nipscern.com ↗
  • Parser: Rust compiled to WebAssembly via wasm-bindgen. Handles XML event files with >100 k cells in under 100 ms on a modern laptop.
  • Renderer: Three.js InstancedMesh with a custom GLSL shader encoding cell energy as luminance, with per-cell highlight on hover.
  • Post-processing: Unreal Bloom pass (subtle) + SMAA anti-aliasing via EffectComposer.
  • Build: Vite + vite-plugin-wasm targeting ESNext. No legacy polyfills.

CERN & the ATLAS Detector

The European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN) operates the Large Hadron Collider — a 27 km superconducting ring in which proton beams are accelerated to 6.8 TeV per beam and collided at four interaction points. [1]

ATLAS (A Toroidal LHC ApparatuS) is one of two general-purpose detectors at the LHC. At 46 m long and approximately 7,000 tonnes, it is the largest detector ever built. ATLAS co-discovered the Higgs boson in July 2012, confirming a prediction of the Standard Model made in 1964. [2]

The central hadronic calorimeter uses interlocked steel absorber plates and plastic scintillating tiles read out by wavelength-shifting fibres and photomultiplier tubes. It surrounds the electromagnetic calorimeter and measures the energy of hadronic showers — cascades of pions and kaons produced when quarks and gluons interact with matter. [3]

Two copper/liquid-argon wheels on each end-cap extend hadronic calorimetry into the forward region. Copper plates serve as passive absorber; liquid argon at 87 K is the active ionisation medium. The signal is induced on copper electrodes by drifting ionisation electrons. [4]

Accordion-folded lead absorbers immersed in liquid argon provide full azimuthal symmetry without projective cracks. The accordion geometry yields fast charge collection and uniform response. It is the primary tool for identifying electrons and photons, with energy resolution σ/E ≈ 10%/√E. [4]

About

CGV Web is developed by the NIPS Signal Processing Laboratory at UFJF (Brazil) in collaboration with the ATLAS experiment at CERN. The viewer uses Three.js, a Rust/WASM XML parser, and custom GLSL shaders. All processing happens locally — no server upload.
